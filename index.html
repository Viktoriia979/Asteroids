Asteroids:

Asteroids are rocky objects that orbit the Sun. They are much smaller than planets and do not have enough gravity to become round like planets. Most asteroids are found in the Asteroid Belt, which is located between Mars and Jupiter. They come in many different sizes, from small rocks to objects hundreds of kilometres wide.

Asteroids formed about 4.6 billion years ago from leftover material after the Solar System was created. They are mainly made of rock, metals such as iron and nickel, and sometimes carbon-rich materials. Scientists believe that asteroids are the remains of objects that never formed into planets because of Jupiter's strong gravity. Studying asteroids helps scientists learn more about the history and formation of the Solar System.

Some famous examples of asteroids include Vesta, Ceres, and Bennu. Vesta is one of the largest asteroids and has a rocky surface with large impact craters. Ceres is the largest object in the Asteroid Belt and is now classified as a dwarf planet. Bennu is a near-Earth asteroid that was visited by NASA's OSIRIS-REx mission, which collected samples to help scientists understand the early Solar System.
